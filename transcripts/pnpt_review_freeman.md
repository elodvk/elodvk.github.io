# How I Conquered the PNPT — A Story of Humility

[section:Introduction]

You know, there's a certain poetry to failure. It teaches you things that success never could. This is the story of how I passed the Practical Network Penetration Tester exam. Not on the first try. No. That would have been too easy. And nothing worth having ever comes easy.

[pause:2]

I passed on September 3rd, 2025. And sitting here now, looking back on it, I can tell you it wasn't about the technical skills. It was about patience. About listening. About learning to get out of your own way.

[pause:2]

[section:Background]

## The Man Who Thought He Knew Everything

I had been working with Active Directory for over eight years. Eight years of managing forests and domains. Recovering entire environments from the ashes. Hardening systems until they were fortresses. I thought I understood networks the way a river understands the land it flows through.

[pause:1.5]

And I had spent a year on Hack The Box. Insane difficulty machines. The kind that make you question whether you belong in this field at all. But I kept going. And I kept winning.

[pause:1]

So when the PNPT came along, an entry-level certification, I looked at it the way a seasoned traveler looks at a short walk. How hard could it be? I skipped the course. I barely prepared. I walked in with nothing but confidence and a year of muscle memory.

[pause:1.5]

That confidence, as it often does, was about to meet humility.

[pause:2]

[section:First Attempt]

## The Fall

The exam presented itself as a real-world penetration test. Five days to compromise a network. Simple enough on paper.

[pause:1]

But the OSINT section. That's where I stumbled. Not because it was impossibly hard. Because I refused to see it for what it was. I was looking for complexity where there was simplicity. I spent two entire days overthinking what should have taken hours.

[pause:1.5]

By day three, I found my way in. A foothold. And for a moment, I felt the rush of progress. But then the rabbit holes appeared. One after another. Each one promising something just around the corner. Each one leading nowhere.

[pause:1]

By day four, I was exhausted. Not physically. Mentally. Spiritually. I stopped. I walked away. And I accepted that this time, this particular chapter, wasn't mine to finish.

[pause:1.5]

There's no shame in that. There's only shame in not coming back.

[pause:2]

[section:Second Attempt]

## The Return

I went back to the drawing board. I built a home lab that mirrored the exam environment. I practiced with new tools. I discovered ligolo-ng for tunneling, which worked beautifully where others had failed me. And most importantly, I changed my approach.

[pause:1.5]

I stopped trying to be clever. I started being thorough.

[pause:1]

When the second attempt came, I reached my previous progress within a single hour. And then, as before, I hit a wall. But this time I didn't panic. I didn't chase. I took a nap.

[pause:1]

When I woke up, I sat down and enumerated. Quietly. Methodically. Like reading a book from the beginning instead of skipping to the end. And there it was. The path forward. It had been there the entire time. I just hadn't been still enough to see it.

[pause:1.5]

One hour later, I was Domain Admin. The network was mine. And I sat there in the quiet of my room, not celebrating. Just breathing. Taking it in.

[pause:1]

The debrief came the next day. Fifteen minutes to present my findings. I hadn't prepared slides. I spoke from memory, from the heart, and finished with twenty seconds remaining. It wasn't graceful. But it was honest. And sometimes that's enough.

[pause:2]

[section:Wisdom]

## What I Learned Along the Way

If I could distill this entire experience into a few truths, they would be these.

[pause:1]

Enumerate. Not quickly. Not impatiently. But thoroughly. The way you would read a letter from someone you love. Every word matters.

[pause:1.5]

Rest when you're tired. Not when you're finished. A clear mind sees what an exhausted one cannot.

[pause:1]

Document everything. Not for the exam. For yourself. Because memory is unreliable, but screenshots are forever.

[pause:1]

Choose your tools with care. A craftsman doesn't blame his tools, but a wise craftsman picks the right ones before he begins.

[pause:1.5]

And perhaps most importantly. Don't skip the preparation. Confidence is valuable. But confidence without preparation is just pride wearing a disguise.

[pause:2]

[section:Closing]

## The Road Ahead

The PNPT taught me something I thought I already knew. That the hardest walls to break through are the ones we build ourselves. Overconfidence. Impatience. The refusal to slow down and look at what's right in front of us.

[pause:1.5]

I passed the exam. But more than that, I learned how to approach a problem. How to fail, and how to return. How to be still when everything inside you wants to rush forward.

[pause:1]

Next comes the OSCP. A longer road. A harder road. But I'm not afraid of it anymore. Because I've learned that the exam isn't the obstacle. I am. And once you understand that, well, everything becomes a little clearer.

[pause:1.5]

Thank you for listening. Take care of yourselves. And remember, the answer is usually right in front of you. You just have to be patient enough to see it.
